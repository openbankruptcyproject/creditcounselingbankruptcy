Credit Counseling for Bankruptcy

Two required courses, explained step by step

Home -- Pre-Filing Course -- Debtor Education -- Find Providers -- Exceptions

Federal law requires every individual who files for bankruptcy to complete two separate courses: one before filing and one after filing. These are not the same course, and you need a certificate from each one. Missing either course can delay or derail your entire case.

This guide explains both courses in plain language: what they involve, how much they cost, how long they take, and where to find approved providers.

The Two Required Courses at a Glance

Requirement Pre-Filing Credit Counseling Post-Filing Debtor Education
Legal basis 11 U.S.C. Section 109(h) 11 U.S.C. Section 111
When Within 180 days before filing After filing, before discharge
Duration 60-90 minutes 60-120 minutes
Cost $15-50 $15-50
Formats Online, phone, in person Online, phone, in person
Certificate Filed with petition or within 14 days Filed before discharge
Consequence of skipping Case dismissed Discharge denied or delayed
Important: These two courses are provided by different categories of approved agencies. The agency that provides your pre-filing credit counseling may or may not also offer the post-filing debtor education course. Make sure you complete both, from approved providers, and keep both certificates.

Course 1: Pre-Filing Credit Counseling

Before you can file a bankruptcy petition under any chapter -- Chapter 7, Chapter 13, Chapter 11, or Chapter 12 -- you must complete a credit counseling session with an agency approved by the U.S. Trustee Program. This requirement was added by the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA) and is codified at 11 U.S.C. Section 109(h).

During the session, a certified counselor will:

  • Review your current financial situation, including income, expenses, assets, and debts
  • Discuss alternatives to bankruptcy, such as debt management plans, negotiation with creditors, or budgeting adjustments
  • Help you understand whether bankruptcy is the right option for your circumstances
  • Develop a personalized budget plan, regardless of whether you proceed with bankruptcy

The session typically takes 60-90 minutes and costs between $15 and $50. Many agencies offer fee waivers for filers who cannot afford the cost. You can complete the session online (at your own pace), by phone, or in person.

Upon completion, you receive a certificate that is valid for 180 days. Your attorney files this certificate with the court along with your bankruptcy petition. If you file without a valid certificate, the court will almost certainly dismiss your case.

For full details on timing, what to expect, and the certificate requirement, see Pre-Filing Credit Counseling: The First Required Course.

Course 2: Post-Filing Debtor Education

After your bankruptcy case is filed, you must complete a second course called "debtor education" or "financial management." This is a separate requirement from the pre-filing credit counseling, and it is governed by a different section of the Bankruptcy Code (11 U.S.C. Section 111).

The debtor education course focuses on practical financial skills:

  • Building and maintaining a household budget
  • Understanding how to use credit responsibly after bankruptcy
  • Money management and financial planning techniques
  • Strategies for rebuilding your financial life after discharge

This course also takes 60-120 minutes, costs $15-50, and can be completed online, by phone, or in person. You will receive a separate certificate upon completion.

Your debtor education certificate must be filed with the court before your discharge can be granted. In a Chapter 7 case, you typically need to complete this course within 60 days of your 341 Meeting of Creditors. In a Chapter 13 case, you must complete it before your final payment or plan completion. If you do not file this certificate, the court may close your case without granting a discharge -- meaning you went through the entire bankruptcy process for nothing.

For a detailed breakdown of what this course covers, see Debtor Education Course: The Second Required Course.

How Much Does It All Cost?

The total cost for both courses is typically $30 to $100. Many providers offer bundle pricing when you purchase both courses upfront:

  • Credit counseling only: $15-50
  • Debtor education only: $15-50
  • Both courses bundled: $30-75

Fee waivers are available from many agencies for filers whose household income is below the federal poverty guidelines. Some agencies offer reduced fees on a sliding scale based on income. If you cannot afford the fee, ask the agency directly -- they are required to provide services regardless of ability to pay.

For a complete breakdown of all bankruptcy costs, including these courses, see bankruptcycost.com.

Online, Phone, or In Person?

Both courses are available in all three formats. Here is how they compare:

Format Pros Cons
Online Available 24/7, self-paced, certificate delivered instantly Less personal interaction, requires internet access
Phone Convenient, live counselor, no computer needed Must schedule during business hours, may involve hold times
In person Face-to-face guidance, best for complex situations Limited availability, travel required

Most filers choose the online option for convenience. Certificates are typically delivered electronically via email within minutes of completing the course, and your attorney can file them with the court immediately.

Finding Approved Providers

Not just any counseling agency will satisfy the bankruptcy requirement. The provider must be specifically approved by the U.S. Trustee Program (or the Bankruptcy Administrator in Alabama and North Carolina districts). The U.S. Department of Justice maintains searchable lists of approved providers at justice.gov.

Be cautious of agencies that charge significantly more than $50 for either course, pressure you into unnecessary services, or are not on the official approved list. For guidance on finding legitimate providers and avoiding scams, see Approved Credit Counseling Providers.

What If You Cannot Complete the Courses?

In rare circumstances, the credit counseling requirement may be waived or temporarily deferred. The Bankruptcy Code provides limited exceptions for:

  • Exigent circumstances that justify filing immediately (Section 109(h)(3))
  • Incapacity -- physical or mental disability that prevents participation
  • Active military service in a combat zone
  • Residence in a district where no approved agencies are available (extremely rare)

These exceptions are narrow and courts interpret them strictly. For details on how to request an exception, see Exceptions to the Credit Counseling Requirement.

Before You File

Credit counseling is just one step in the bankruptcy filing process. Before you file, you should also understand:

  • Eligibility: Are you eligible to file again? Check your eligibility at 1328f.com/before-you-file.html.
  • Costs: What will the entire process cost? See bankruptcycost.com for a complete breakdown of filing fees, attorney fees, and hidden costs.
  • Your attorney: Before hiring a bankruptcy attorney, check their track record. 1328f.com's free attorney checker shows how their clients' cases actually turned out across all 94 federal districts.
Pro tip: Complete your pre-filing credit counseling early in the process -- ideally as soon as you start seriously considering bankruptcy. The certificate is valid for 180 days, so there is no downside to completing it early, and it removes one item from your pre-filing checklist.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is credit counseling for bankruptcy?

Credit counseling for bankruptcy is a mandatory course that every individual must complete before filing for bankruptcy under any chapter. Required by 11 U.S.C. Section 109(h), it involves a session with an approved nonprofit counseling agency that reviews your financial situation, discusses alternatives to bankruptcy, and helps you create a budget. The session typically takes 60-90 minutes and costs $15-50, with fee waivers available for low-income filers.

How many courses are required for bankruptcy?

Two separate courses are required: (1) pre-filing credit counseling, which must be completed within 180 days before your bankruptcy petition is filed, and (2) post-filing debtor education (also called financial management), which must be completed after filing but before your discharge is granted. These are different courses offered by different types of approved providers, and you need a separate certificate for each one.

How much does bankruptcy credit counseling cost?

Pre-filing credit counseling typically costs $15-50. The post-filing debtor education course also costs $15-50. Many providers offer both courses as a bundle for $30-75. Fee waivers or reduced fees are available for filers who cannot afford the cost. Some nonprofit agencies offer free counseling to those below the federal poverty guidelines.

Can I take credit counseling online?

Yes. Both the pre-filing credit counseling course and the post-filing debtor education course can be completed online, by phone, or in person. Online courses are the most popular option and are available 24/7 from most approved providers. You will receive your certificate electronically upon completion, which your attorney can file with the court immediately.

What happens if I do not complete credit counseling before filing?

If you file a bankruptcy petition without a valid credit counseling certificate, the court will likely dismiss your case. In very limited circumstances, such as exigent circumstances that merit a waiver under 11 U.S.C. Section 109(h)(3), you may receive a temporary exemption and have up to 30 days after filing to complete the course. However, these exemptions are rarely granted, and the safest approach is to complete credit counseling before filing.
